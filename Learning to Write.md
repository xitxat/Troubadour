# **LEARNING TO WRITE — MASTER OVERVIEW**

This file teaches *how* writing works.
It is not for producing prose; it is for understanding:

* techniques
* structures
* terminology
* decisions a writer makes
* and how historical novelists create engagement

This gives you the intellectual framework so that research and reading become instructive, not just inspirational.

---

# **1. The Three Pillars of Your Learning Path**

Everything you study in the early months falls under one of these three pillars:

## **1. Narrative Structure**

How stories are *architected* beneath the surface.

Key terms you must know:

* **Braided narrative**
* **Parallel structure**
* **Interleaving**
* **Dual timeline / multi-timeline**
* **Nested stories**
* **Convergent plotlines**
* **Scene → sequel pattern**
* **Turning points**
* **Act structure (3-act, 5-act, dramatic arc)**
* **Discovery plot** (Clarke’s *Rama*)
* **Epic braid** (Hancock’s *War God*)

This pillar teaches how writers weave multiple threads and keep readers engaged through rhythm, pacing, contrast, and convergence.

---

## **2. Point of View & Voice**

How your narrator perceives, describes, and interprets the world.

Terms to study:

* **Focalization**
  – Internal (reader knows what narrator knows)
  – External (camera-like observation)
* **Psychic distance** (Gardner’s scale 1–5)
* **Unreliable narrator**
* **Interior monologue**
* **Filter words** (and how to reduce them)
* **Embodied POV**
* **Voice-patterning** (Murderbot’s secret weapon)
* **Lens principle** (the narrator’s worldview shaping every detail)

This pillar dictates how intimate or distant your narrative feels — especially crucial in historical fiction where worldview, culture, and social norms must filter through your narrator.

---

## **3. Engagement Mechanics**

How you *hold* a reader’s attention, even if they didn't think they cared about your subject.

Terms to study:

* **Micro-tension** (Donald Maass)
* **Scene beats**
* **Scene “turns”**
* **Status dynamics / status transactions**
* **Subtext**
* **Curiosity hooks**
* **Mystery scaffolding**
* **Character yearning**
* **Contradiction-driven character design**
* **Emotional stakes vs historical stakes**

This pillar is how *Downton Abbey* grabs audiences who don’t care about Edwardian culture — through relational tension, status play, secrets, and power shifts.

# **2. Deep-Dive Study Areas**

Below are the domains you will study step-by-step, with terminology included.

---

## **2.1 Braided / Interwoven Narrative Forms**

This is your strongest interest, so we treat it as a main discipline.

### **Terms to know**

* **Braided narrative**
* **Parallel narrative**
* **Alternating chapters**
* **Intercuts / interleaving**
* **Thematic echoing**
* **Structural rhythm (A → B → A → C pattern)**
* **Convergent midpoint**
* **Convergent climax**
* **Counterpoint scenes** (scenes in different threads that contrast each other deliberately)

### **Skills you will learn**

* Designing multiple threads that escalate independently
* Controlling scene-switching timing
* Using clifflets (mini-cliffhangers) to maintain momentum
* Creating resonance across threads
* Balancing internal vs external stakes
* Using timeline offset (past/present) to reveal information strategically

### **References**

* War God (Hancock) — epic braid
* Downton Abbey — ensemble braid
* Dual timelines in historical novels (e.g., *The Nightingale*, *All the Light We Cannot See*)
* Investigative structures (Rama)

## **2.2 First-Person Voice — Craft Skills Needed**

You want to use first-person.
First-person is powerful, but technical.

### **Terms to study**

* **Psychic distance**
* **Focalization**
* **Internal monologue**
* **Subjective filtering**
* **Unreliable narration**
* **Narrative persona**
* **Embodied description**
* **Self-interruption** (Murderbot technique)
* **Narrative time (now vs then voice)**

### **Skills you will learn**

* How to give a narrator a unique personality
* How to reveal the world through the narrator’s worldview
* How to balance emotion with observation
* How to use internal monologue to create tension
* How to maintain clarity while staying deep in the narrator’s head

---

## **2.3 Scene Craft — the Micro-Level Mechanics**

A novel is built of scenes. Nothing else.

### **Terms to learn**

* **Goal → conflict → outcome**
* **Scene beats** (micro-shifts)
* **Scene Turn** (what changes?)
* **Reversal**
* **Tactical action** (what character *does* physically/emotionally)
* **Subtext**
* **Status play**
* **Stakes (surface, emotional, thematic)**

### **Skills you will develop**

* Designing scenes that matter
* Ensuring every scene changes something
* Embedding micro-tension even in “quiet” scenes
* Making dialogue do more than convey information
* Creating emotional contrast to shape pacing

---

## **2.4 Historical Immersion Without Overload**

Historical fiction dies when writers dump research into the prose.

### **Terms to study**

* **Contextual exposition**
* **Material culture**
* **Period worldview**
* **Historical texture**
* **Diegetic detail** (details the character naturally notices)
* **Cultural triangulation** (showing differences in perspective)
* **Integration pacing**
* **Relevance filtering** (only include details that affect the present moment)

### **Skills you will learn**

* How to signal a historical world without dumping encyclopaedia pages
* How to embed research into behaviour and social rules
* How to reveal history through conflict
* How to avoid anachronistic thinking
* How to design a research pipeline (archives, reading, annotation)

# **3. The “Before Writing” Workflow You Want**

Here is the pre-writing preparation used by professional historical novelists:

## **3.1 Macro Level**

1. **Choose your structural model**
   (braided, dual timeline, linear with flashbacks, etc.)

2. **Identify your core thematic axis**
   (e.g., faith vs conquest, loyalty vs survival, honour vs truth)

3. **Design your threads**
   Each thread = goal + stakes + emotional wound.

4. **Map their convergence points**
   Midpoint or final 20%.

5. **Research dossier**
   Culture, politics, daily life, material objects, social hierarchies.

## **3.2 Micro Level**

6. **Define narrator’s voice & psychic distance level**
   (close, mid, or shifting)

7. **Scene types you will use**
   (tension scenes, reveal scenes, relationship scenes, discovery scenes)

8. **Scene checklist**
   – Does something change?
   – Is there micro-tension?
   – Is history embedded in behaviour, not info-dumping?
